About Save the Karoo

This website serves to inform everybody about events as they are experienced on the ground, surrounding SKA, the Square Kilometer Array radio astronomy project in South Africa's Karoo.

What is the Karoo?

The Karoo is a semi-desert spanning approximately 400,000 square km in the geographic midriff of South Africa. There is no exact definition of what constitutes the Karoo, and therefore its extent is also not precisely defined. The Karoo is partly defined by its topography, geology, and climate â€” above all, its low rainfall, arid air, cloudless skies, and extremes of heat and cold. The Karoo also hosted a well-preserved ecosystem hundreds of million years ago which is now represented by many fossils. Wikipedia 2016.

What is SKA?

SKA or Square Kilometer Array is the worlds largest radio astronomy project that is being set up in Australia and South Africa. Multiple radio dishes are to be linked up in order to obtain a total collection area of approximately one square kilometre. CSIR 2016.

In our main research article 'SKA is ruining the Karoo's future', South Africa's chosen site will be examined, many immediate and future impacts considered and financial challenges facing the project will be highlighted.

Affected and interested parties are invited to share comments and stories on our Facebook page or sign our online petition. Please feel free to ask questions and become involved.

A big thank you to all the wonderful people of the Karoo, and our friends from further afield, who have all worked so hard to help create this website. The support and love you have shown will save the Karoo.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it not too late to stop SKA (Square Kilometer Array) development?

No, currently SKA owns 13 406 ha and only have precursors and pathfinders constructed on a small area of this ground. Most of this land lies vacant. SKA still want to buy much more land in order to expand. The big part of the SKA project, SKA phase 1 has not yet been constructed. It is only planned for 2018 and it will take many years to complete. Now is the right time to question the chosen site for SKA's further development.

How much land does SKA want to buy? And how will they use the land?

In addition to the 13 406 ha, SKA want to buy up a further 118 000 ha for SKA phase 1. Radio satellite dishes will be erected on the core area of the intended 132 000 ha and a large "silent zone" is planned around the satellite dishes.

Why is it stated that SKA is preventing a civilised life in the area?

An SKA expert explained the regulations and resulting restrictions placed on the Astronomically Advantaged Area ( AAA) within which SKA lies as follows: "You may not enter or reside in this region without permission from the management authority. You may not do most things compatible with civilised life - such as erect lights, fly an airplane, drive a truck, construct a road, build a house, or broadcast a radio signal - in this region without permission. Even such farm-like activities as combine harvesting and arc-welding will be severely restricted or prohibited."

Later the same expert added more restrictions on generating power (even wind energy), spark plugs (petrol driven vehicles included), electric fencing, digital mobile equipment like hand held devices and other electric equipment. The are also restrictions on traffic and access to some roads is controlled.

How large is the AAA (Astronomically Advantaged Area)?

The AAA is 14,000,000 ha large. It covers the central Karoo area, and includes all the municipal areas of the Northern Cape excluding the Sol Plaatje municipality.

How many people will be affected by SKA ?

SKA phase 1 will affect three municipal areas where 46 000 people live. SKA phases 2 and 3 will affect many in the Karoo area and also a substantial number of the 1,2 million inhabitants living in the Northern Cape.

Which negative impact caused by SKA will affect the most people?

The modification to radio signals affecting cell phone reception will upset most people. Mobile connectivity will be lost. Up to the minute news, information, and communication will leave the public feeling cut off from the rest of the world. Many businesses will suffer. Many people will loose their only chance of Internet access.

What is SKA's biggest problem?

Funding, is the greatest challenge facing SKA. More information about monetary issues can be found in our article's funding section.

Why did SKA choose the central Karoo area for a site?

This question is difficult to answer with certainty. SKA calls the Karoo a "remote desert" and "radio quiet", but South Africans know that the Karoo is a semi-desert, with great agricultural value. If the Karoo were sufficiently radio quiet, why is mobile connectivity currently being lost? One SKA official did indicate that a true desert would be too cost prohibitive for such a project. Obviously, existing infrastructures in the respective municipal areas have made it cheaper for SKA to construct the pathfinders and precursor satellite dishes in the area.
